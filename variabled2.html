Credit Card Use and Age:

By analyzing the bar graph, we can see that within the “not at all concerned” group, the majority (47%) of respondents come from the 16-34 year old age demographic.

Within the “very concerned” group, the opposite trend is shown. The majority of respondents (60%) fall within the 45+ age demographic.

The “concerned” group takes on more of a transitional pattern between the “not at all concerned” and the “very concerned”, as the data is shows a relatively normal distribution.

Looking at the information and patterns shown in both the frequency distribution table and the bar graph, it is evident that the data shown supports our hypothesis, which states that concern with online financial systems increases with age.

The ‘Age’ variable in this case would represent a discrete random variable, simply because of the fact that although the data is categorized within an interval, the value must be an integer, i.e. the age value cannot be ‘16.7’. For example, within the interval of 16-24, the data variable ‘Age’ could be represented by any numerical integer value between 16-24, thereby classifying it as a discrete random variable.

As seen in the frequency distribution, within the 3 age demographics ‘16-24’, ‘25-34’, ‘35-44’ the observed values were all higher than the expected values in the ‘Not at all concerned’ category. Whilst on the other hand, in the ‘Very concerned’ category the observed values were lower than the expected. Overall, this shows that within these 3 age demographics, the data supports our original hypothesis that younger users have less concern with online credit card usage, less users are ‘very concerned’ and more fall within the ‘not at all concerned’ categories.

The opposite trend occurs in the 3 remaining age demographics of ‘45-54’, ‘55-64’, and ‘65+’. The values seen in the ‘very concerned’ were higher than expected, and those in the ‘not at all concerned’ were lower than expected, once again supporting our original hypothesis.

If we isolate and look at the age variable by itself, we can find the following calculations:

  • Mean: 3.58
  • Variance: 2.83
  • Std Dev: 1.68

Credit Card Use and Income:

In this case, the variable ‘income’ can be considered as a continuous random variable, since it may be represented by any numerical value between the specified intervals. For example, within the first quintile, the random variable ‘income’ may take on any numerical value between $0 and $25,000.

As evident in the frequency distribution table, the lower income quintiles show a higher than expected value within the “very concerned” category. The opposite trend can also be seen in the higher income quintiles, where the “very concerned” category shows lower than expected values. This also supports our original hypothesis that respondents with higher income would be less concerned with online credit card usage and transactions, and lower income groups would show higher levels of concern.

Furthermore, as this survey collects information from a specific sample group, which in turn represents the general public as a population. Therefore, we can apply the law of large numbers and assume that as the sample size increases, its sample mean will approach the population mean as well.

If we isolate and look at the income variable by itself, we can find the following calculations:

  • Mean: 3.26
  • Variance: 1.92
  • Std Dev: 1.38

Using these calculations, we can then find out the z-score for any given ‘income’ value within the dataset. The Z-score represents how many standard deviations the data value is from the mean of the sample, under the assumption that the data falls under the normal distribution category.

If we use, for example, the income value 4, which represent the interval ‘$70,000-$100,000’, we can find using the following formula that the Z-score is 0.536, meaning it is 0.536 standard deviations away from the mean of 3.26.
